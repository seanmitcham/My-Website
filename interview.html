Interview Preparation

Below are my responses to common interview questions for the IT / business field I am pursuing.

1. What development tools have you used?

In my coursework I've worked with basic development and coding environments like VS Code, mostly for building websites with HTML and CSS. I've also used GitHub to manage and publish projects. At my internship I work more with support and administration tools rather than development, but I understand how the two connect since a lot of IT support involves scripting and system configuration.

2. What programming languages have you used?

Right now my main experience is with HTML and CSS from my web development coursework. I'm building on that as I go through my CIS program, and I'm comfortable picking up new languages quickly since a lot of the logic carries over once you understand the fundamentals.

3. What technical certifications do you hold?

I currently hold CompTIA A+, CompTIA Network+, and CompTIA Server+. These have given me a strong foundation in hardware, networking, and server environments, which comes in handy every day at my internship.

4. How do you maintain your technical certifications?

I stay on top of renewal requirements and continuing education credits through CompTIA, and I try to keep learning outside of class through hands-on practice at my internship. Technology changes fast, so I make it a point to stay current rather than just letting a certification sit on my resume.

5. How is your education helping prepare you for the job market?

Double majoring in Computer Information Systems and Business Administration gives me both the technical background and the business side of things, which I think makes me more well rounded than someone who only has one or the other. My cybersecurity concentration especially lines up with where I want to take my career, and my finance concentration helps me understand the business decisions that go along with IT work.

6. How would you rate your key competencies for this position?

I'd say my strongest competencies are troubleshooting, communication, and adaptability. I'm used to walking into a situation I haven't seen before and figuring it out, whether that's a hardware issue at work or a new leadership challenge with my fraternity. I'm always working to build on my technical skills too, especially in networking and security.

7. What are your IT strengths and weaknesses?

My strengths are hands-on troubleshooting, customer service, and staying calm under pressure when something needs to get fixed quickly. Something I'm still working on is deeper hands-on experience with programming and scripting, since most of my background so far has been on the support and networking side. I'm actively working to close that gap through my coursework and by picking up projects on my own.

8. Describe a recent project and your responsibilities.

One recent project was building this portfolio website from scratch using HTML. I had to plan out five separate pages, make sure the navigation worked correctly across all of them, and organize my resume, goals, and experience into something that looks professional. It was a good reminder of how much planning goes into something that looks simple on the surface.

9. What challenges might you expect in this position?

Coming from an internship into a full-time IT role, I'd expect the biggest challenge to be scaling up from supporting one hospital's systems to a larger or more complex environment. I'd also expect to run into new tools and processes I haven't used before, but I see that as something I can adjust to quickly based on how I've picked things up at NRMC.

10. Why is it important to work directly with business users?

IT only works if it actually solves the problems people are dealing with day to day. Working directly with business users helps me understand what they actually need instead of just guessing, and it makes it easier to explain technical issues in a way that makes sense to someone who isn't in IT. At the hospital, that means keeping the technology running smoothly so the staff can focus on patient care instead of worrying about their computers.
